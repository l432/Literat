The paper reviews various methods for detecting defects in solar modules, with a primary focus on techniques involving image analysis obtained through different approaches. While this is an intriguing idea, its implementation lacks sufficient depth. Most of the issues highlighted in the review are addressed too briefly, necessitating frequent reference to the cited sources. Additionally, the information provided does not offer significant novelty compared to other published reviews. 
In my opinion, the manuscript should be rejected.

Furthermore, I would like to point out several specific shortcomings:

1.The manuscript claims that Fig. 1 is taken from reference [2]. However, the image in the original source does not match the one presented in the manuscript. Moreover, the description of this figure ("The operation of photovoltaic installations can be divided into three categories: those connected to the grid and those operating as stand-alone systems." – see page 2, lines 9–10) does not align with the content of the figure.

2.The text attributes Fig. 2 to reference [3]. However, the "hierarchy of potential faults" depicted in Fig. 2 differs significantly from the version proposed by the authors of reference [3].

3.The manuscript includes a section titled "Proposed Structure for the Review." This is an excellent idea, as it provides readers with a clear understanding of the author's intentions. However, this section is positioned after the first section, which "provides an overview of the various types of photovoltaic systems, categorized according to their installation and connection" (page 3, lines 4–5). It is worth noting that this overview is very superficial.

4.The manuscript also states, "The visual inspection process...is more accurate and effective than other available techniques [22]" (page 5, lines 45–48). First, this claim is highly controversial. Second, this statement is not supported by reference [22], which only mentions that "visual inspection is the quickest and most convenient way to detect defects in a solar panel" or that "visual inspection is an inexpensive method."

5.In Section 2, visual inspection and current-voltage (I-V) curve analysis are categorized as distinct approaches to defect detection. However, in Section 3.1, it is stated that one variation of visual techniques involves the analysis of I-V curves, creating inconsistency.

6.How do "aerial images" differ from "visual inspection"? According to Fig. 3, these are presented as distinct methods for characterizing photovoltaic systems.

7. Section 3.2 occupies approximately one-third of the manuscript and describes various methods for obtaining images of photovoltaic modules. However, this information is entirely unoriginal and has been published multiple times before. For example, the beginning of Section 3.2.2 closely resembles the Section 5.5 in [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.rser.2021.111532]. Additionally, Figures 5, 9, 11, 13, and 16 in the manuscript are analogous to figures from the referenced work. While the authors cite the original source, one must question the purpose of republishing widely available information.

Furthermore, both the reviewed manuscript (Fig. 9) and [https://doi.org/10.1016/j.rser.2021.111532] (Fig. 14) refer to the necessity of a filter that is purportedly shown in the figure. However, the filter is absent in both cases, suggesting that errors are being propagated as well.

8. The classifications of defects in photovoltaic systems provided in the Introduction and Section 4 differ from each other. The authors should consider adopting a single, consistent classification. 

9. The manuscript contains unclear abbreviations, such as "PNK" in the caption of Fig. 25, which are not explained.

10. Section 4 discusses various types of degradation and failure modes in photovoltaic module components, while Section 3 focuses on detecting faults in photovoltaic modules using visual inspection techniques. However, there is no clear connection between these sections. Specifically, the manuscript does not indicate which detection methods are suitable for identifying particular types of defects.

11. Section 5 is notably problematic. It comprises only a single paragraph and a table. The paragraph, containing fewer than 300 words, provides general information about the application of machine learning across different industries and presents a classification of AI models for diagnosing the degradation of solar modules and cells. However, the classification is confusing, as it is unclear how the third category of methods differs from the second.

It is also stated that "Table 2 presents a comprehensive overview of the existing literature on... modes through the application of artificial intelligence (AI) models" (by the way,, the introduction of the abbreviation AI in the last line of the manuscript warrants special attention). I would respectfully disagree with the epithet "comprehensive" and suggest that the authors consider the following reviews:

https://doi.org/10.1109/JPHOTOV.2022.3221003 
https://doi.org/10.1109/ACCESS.2023.3298542 
These works focus on the application of machine learning in photovoltaics in general, but they also contain a significant amount of information on defect detection.
